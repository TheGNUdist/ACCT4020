Becoming a Professional

Before Knowledge

Before we begin our discussions of information, technology, and control, I wish to impress upon your minds the attitudes necessary to successfully transition from student to professional. In this course, I can impart knowledge (if, and only if, you are willing to receive it), but the metamorphosis from student to professional involves more than the acquisition of core competencies. Professionalism is a commitment to a way of life.

Reasons for Fraud

The Fraud Triangle highlights the three necessary conditions for fraud to occur: pressure (e.g., financial or peer), opportunity, and rationalization (i.e., the belief that the fraud was justified). Later in the course, we will discuss controls as a means to reduce opportunity, but only internal commitment can prevent rationalization. Professionalism is the embodiment of that commitment.

Why Professionalism Matters

In the physical world, if rules are disregarded, things won't work. It is the same in society.
– Dr. Irv Tankersley

Let us review some examples of the consequences of unprofessional behavior...

Fraud No. 1

Nick Leeson: Chief trader for Barings Bank (est. 1762)

Choices
  • Made large unauthorized speculative trades.
  • Used an error account to hide losses to preserve his reputation as a trading star.
  • Made increasingly risky trades to recover hidden losses.
Consequences
  • Accumulated trading losses of £827 million; twice the bank's available trading capital.
  • Served four years in a Singaporian prison.
  • Bank collapse cost an additional £100 million.
  • Bank declared insolvent and sold to ING for £1.

Fraud No. 2

Jeffrey Skilling: CEO for Enron

Choices
  • Created more than 500 Special Purpose Entities to book fake revenues and hide Enron's liabilities.
  • Colluded with chairman, CFO and auditor, Arthur Andersen.
Consequences
  • Arthur Andersen could no longer audit public companies.
  • Sentenced to 24 years in prison.
  • Largest Chapter 11 bankruptcy in history.

Fraud No. 3

Bernard Ebbers: CEO of WorldCom

Choices
  • Colluded with CFO, Controller and Director of General Accounting:
    • Understated expenses by $7 billion by booking line costs as capital expenditures.
    • Overstated revenues by $1 billion by fabricating one-time items.
Consequences
  • Serves a 25-year prison sentence.
  • New largest Chapter 11 bankruptcy.
  • Debtors received 35¢ on the dollar; Investors received 0.
  • Largest accounting fraud in American history.

Fraud No. 4

Bernie Madoff: Bernard L. Madoff Investment Securities LLC

Choices
  • Started a brokerage firm in 1960 to trade stocks in unlisted companies.
  • Amassed the world's largest hedge fund with over $3 billion.
  • One day, he stopped trading...
  • ...but he never stopped attracting new investors.
Consequences
  • Turned in by his own sons, one of whom subsequently committed suicide.
  • Sentenced to 150 years in prison.
  • Largest private Ponzi scheme in history and new largest accounting fraud in American history.

Professionalism Defined

The skill, judgment, and polite behavior that is expected from a person who is trained to do a job well; the conduct, aims, or qualities that mark a profession or a professional person.
– Merriam-Webster

Professional Traits

The AICPA Code of Professional Conduct lists the following Principles of Professional Conduct:

  • Responsibilities
  • The Public Interest
  • Integrity
  • Objectivity and Independence
  • Due Care
  • Scope and Nature of Services

These principles highlight five professional characteristics: diligence, competence, responsibility, integrity and objectivity.

Diligence

Patton

Diligence vs. Prioritization

Laziness will achieve nothing, but diligence is most fruitful when focused on the best activities. We only ever accomplish a goal when we invest in that goal. Diligently pursuing a distraction is not a substitute.

Furthermore, only the pursuit of valuable goals is valuable. Even the most dedicated pursuit of a useless goal (e.g., mastering Guitar Hero on the most difficult setting) is useless.

Competence

Although you should always work to develop each of these traits, this one is the most relevant for you right now. Competence is knowledge, and each professional must possess a common body of knowledge. The active acquisition of this body of knowledge requires diligence, reasoning and accreditation. Fogelman provides the latter, but you are responsible for the former. My role is to support you, but I cannot teach you, unless you choose to learn.

Responsibility

A Christmas Carol

Choices Have Consequences

One of the most important lessons from any public fraud or scandal is that choices have consequences. Take responsibility for yourselves, as well as others.

Integrity and Objectivity

  • Tell the truth.
  • Never withhold relevant information in order to mislead others.
  • Consider all the facts, even when they disagree with your opinions.
  • Keep commitments.
  • Stand for something.

Two Sides of the Triangle

Nick Leeson would never have been able to accumulate sufficient losses to bring down an entire bank if he had told the truth after the first bad trade. Interestingly, professionalism would have reduced both pressure and rationalization.

Respect

This is not a focus of the AICPA Code of Professional Conduct, but polite behavior is a part of the definition of professionalism, and respect is clearly a relevant component of polite behavior.

To Sir, With Love

Four Aspects of Proper Comportment

  1. Proper speech: Use correct grammar; avoid slang, profanity and filler words; refrain from interrupting.
  2. Correct address: Avoid first names without permission; use the highest title.
  3. Professional dress: Suit, long-sleeved, button-down shirt and tie for males; pants or skirt suit, button-down blouse and closed-toe shoes for females. These are the uniform of the professional.
  4. Humility: Refrain from judging others; never use peer performance to excuse behavior.

Not the Bear

One rationale for using peer performance as an excuse is the belief that success in life involves not being eaten by a bear.

When being chased by a bear, it is not necessary to outrun the bear, just the person next to you.

The Lion and the Gazelle

The life of a professional is not about being second-worst, but life will involve running.

Every morning in Africa, a gazelle wakes up, it knows it must outrun the fastest lion or it will be killed. Every morning in Africa, a lion wakes up. It knows it must run faster than the slowest gazelle, or it will starve. It doesn't matter whether you're the lion or a gazelle when the sun comes up, you'd better be running.
