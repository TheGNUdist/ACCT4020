Accounting Architecture

What Is an Accountant?

Accounting textbooks teach us that accounting is the measurement and communication of economic activities. This makes accountants the custodians of business information. Most accounting courses teach regulations to prepare students to become staff accountants or auditors or tax preparers. Although these are valuable skills, the role of information custodianship is much broader than tax returns, journal entries, and ledgers. Information custodianship involves understanding how to create information from the data that financial and non-financial business transactions generate, and accounting employers have begun to realize the need for a broader perspective of the definition of an accountant.

Let Me Break It Down

Accountants

measure and communicate

economic events

by collecting relevant data

into an information system

and converting it

into useful information

for internal and external stakeholders.

Necessary Competencies

This accounting role has three main components:

  • Data collection: the creation and acquisition of data.
  • Data analytics: the discovery of business information.
  • Data visualization: the reporting of business information to decision makers.

Accounting employers have also explicitly identified these core competencies as skills they look for in future hires. Because data collection brings data into an information system and because data analytics and visualization convert data in an information system into useful information, a clear understanding of the components and functions of an information system also becomes a necessary competency for all accountants.

Either Side of the Table

Proficiency in data collection, analytics, and visualization are vital regardless of the type of work an accountant performs. Financial accountants and tax accountants file different reports, but they both use information systems to collect, analyze, and report. Managerial accountants focus on internal, as opposed to external, reports and stakeholders, but they need the same skills to perform the same tasks with the same information system. Even auditors, who are only interested in the accuracy of the reports and not in the information in the reports, use the client's information system to collect, analyze, and visualize client data. Incidentally, they also need an information system to report their findings.

Nothing New Under the Sun

The importance of understanding business data and the information system is not new to the accounting profession. For many decades, digital information systems have played a central role in supporting accountants in their data collection, analytics, and visualization tasks. More recently, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 mandated the evaluation and audit of client information systems by external auditors to verify that they meet control objectives to maintain data integrity and prevent unauthorized access. However, certain trends have caused employers to place a renewed emphasis on acquiring data collection, analytics, and visualization skills and information systems expertise, making it even more important for accountants to focus on developing, retaining, and honing these skills.

Information Is Money

What has precipitated a renewed desire for these skills? Big Data. The world creates more than 2.5 quintillion bytes of data daily (1 quintillion bytes ≈ 1 exabyte ≈ 1 billion gigabytes) and the rate of data creation will continue to grow as more individuals use Internet-connected smart devices and as more individuals contribute in ever-increasing amounts of data on social media. The data growth rate is so extreme that despite the birth of the modern Internet being “way back” in the 1970s, more than 90% of all data is less than 5 years old. Understanding this data unlocks insights into customer behavior, economic trends, business strategies, etc.

He uses his power and wealth to gain information. The more he acquires, the greater his power and wealth. – Sherlock Holmes on Charles Magnussen

The Information Era

The pursuit of information is the very reason for the proliferation of digital devices and information systems. Technological innovation is certainly cool, but technology is not an end to itself. All technology serves to access existing data or to generate new data. This phenomenon does not only apply to business. We as individuals experience it every day. We use Internet-connected devices as our personal portal into a plethora of information systems to read, watch, browse, search, buy, post, and follow. Each of these interactions accesses data or generates data, and most of them do both. Streaming videos creates data about what we like to watch. Reading news articles creates data about what topics interest us. That is how we all use information systems every day.

All Types of Information?

Not all data that we create and access on a day-to-day basis immediately looks like accounting data, nor does it obviously relate to financial statements or tax forms. That does not mean that it is not accounting data or that accountants don't need to know about or care about it. Accounting education focuses primarily on accounting regulations, such as GAAP for financial accounting or GAAS for auditing, but accounting is much more than regulations. The largest consumer of accounting information is management, and management cares about any data and any information that can increase the firm's income and wealth. Accountants measure and communicate economic events to stakeholders, even if it comes from marketing data, HR data, finance data, or operations data. Accountants are custodians of all types of business data.

Why Can't Things Be the Way They Were Before?

Talk of collecting, analyzing, and visualizing all types of business data and understanding information systems may seem like a significant addition to the expectations placed on accountants, and, in fact, accountants as a group have not done a great job of filling these roles. For example, because accountants have a limited understanding of information systems, they have been poorly equipped to respond to past legislation requiring them to audit the reliability of existing information systems. The more technology- and data-driven the business world becomes, the more important these skills will be. Accountants need to keep up, or they risk being left behind.

Where It All Starts

Data collection, analytics, and visualization begin with a well-functioning accounting information system. Without the information system, there is no data, no information, no reporting. Because these functions begin with the information system, mastery of these competencies also begins with an understanding of the purposes, design, and functionality of information systems. Understanding the information system allows accountants to ensure that the correct data is collected, to verify that the data is stored properly and securely so that it will be available when it is needed, and to identify and use the technologies and tools that enable accountants to analyze the data and convert it into valuable information.

Accounting Architecture

Understanding information systems begins with a framework that summarizes and explains the objectives, inputs, outputs, and makeup of an information system. Accounting architecture is a unifying framework of accounting information and the system that supplies that information. An understanding of accounting architecture prepares accountants to become information custodians. The arch displayed here provides a graphical representation of the components of accounting architecture. The arch comprises four sections: Environment, Information, Technology, and Control.

Why Architecture?

Many disciplines have concepts of the structure, building blocks, and form of a system, and they call these “architecture” in the same sense that a physical structure has an architecture. Business architecture is the design of business models and activities to fulfill business strategies. Software architecture is the structure and design of computer software. Systems architecture is the design of computer systems. Accounting architecture relies heavily on systems architecture, and as such, could be called accounting systems architecture. However, this is too limiting a label. Accounting architecture is the structure of accounting information, as well as the structure of the information system necessary to capture, record, and communicate that information.

Environment

The Environment is the collection of external and internal forces that affect the business entity and its accounting information:

  1. Compliance
  2. Business Model
  3. Risk

These environmental constructs determine the boundaries of a useful information system. Think of these as the foundation of a house. The foundation is not sufficient to determine the best building schematics, but it can exclude many incompatible blueprints. The environment dictates certain requirements for the information system, and the other sections of the arch demonstrate how to design a system that meets those requirements.

Compliance

Compliance deals with the entity's regulatory environment. The main focus of many accounting courses is rules and regulations. Financial accounting, audit, and tax are rules-based, and any useful information system must generate information necessary to comply with these rules (e.g., financial statements, tax forms, etc.). Federal, state, and local laws are also part of the regulatory environment.

Compliance also addresses contractual relationships with lenders, suppliers, and customers. Although these are not regulatory bodies, they may demand access to certain information or the implementation of certain controls. Satisfying contractual requirements can be as important as satisfying regulatory requirements in order to avoid legal liability, fines, and lost business.

Business Model

The business model is the form the business entity takes to achieve its long-term strategies. This can include the chosen industry to operate in, ownership structure, financing method, investments, etc. The business model also comprises a collection of business processes that the entity uses to accomplish vital activities, such as purchasing assets from vendors, providing goods and services to customers, or paying employees. These business processes trigger economic events that generate business data. Accountants collect, analyze, and visualize this data in order to generate information for decision makers. One goal of this information is to provide insights into potential modifications of the current model, as well as entirely new strategies. (Big Data analytics has been particularly useful for divining new business strategies.)

Risk

Any business environment contains both internal and external risks. External risks include general economic conditions, industry competition and developments, availability of financing and financing requirements, litigation, technological developments, and digital threats (e.g., cyber incidents). Internal risks include business expansion, management ability and core competencies, corporate governance and culture, changes in key personnel, resource capacity, fraud, data loss and error, and technological feasibility of business and IT objectives. These risks influence the types of data that the accountants should collect and analyze and, more importantly, also the set of controls the business entity should implement to prevent these risks from threatening profitability.

Information

The Information section of the arch comprises three blocks:

  1. Creation: data collection or acquisition and entry into the system.
  2. Use: data analysis and visualization.
  3. Maintenance: data storage and preservation.

Each of these three blocks contains multiple steps. Together they form the information life cycle, which dictates what an information system should do to convert data into information. Although Use is the culminating block of both the Information section and the architecture model as a whole, knowledge of data analysis tools is incomplete without an understanding of the data and the process by which it becomes information.

Technology

The Technology section comprises four blocks:

  1. Hardware: physical components of the information system.
  2. Software: operating systems and applications.
  3. Storage: physical and logical data stores.
  4. Services: networking and cloud computing.

The Technology blocks represent the physical manifestation of the information system (i.e., what the information system should be). Without these technologies, the information system cannot do anything; without an understanding of these technologies, accountants cannot ensure the “black box” of the information system will collect necessary data and generate necessary information.

Solutions ≠ Tools

When selecting technologies to integrate into an information system, it is popular to ask, “How can I achieve solution X with tool Y?” (e.g., How can I track sales [X] in Microsoft Access [Y]?) However, this attitude leads to an overly restrictive view of possible solutions to business problems, especially in situations when individuals have familiarity with only a small set of tools (e.g., Y always equals Microsoft Excel). This phenomenon is known as the Law of the Instrument: If all you have is a hammer, every problem looks like a nail. Posing this question in multiple parts—“How can I achieve solution X?” and “Which tool(s) can best help me to achieve solution X?”—redirects attention away from tools and toward solutions. Because the solution is the actual goal, focusing on solutions instead of tools can result in superior business outcomes.

What About Tools?

Even when focusing on solutions, an information system must, in the end, comprise specific technological tools, and the users of the information system must understand those tools in order to derive value from them. As a result, a grasp of accounting architecture is incomplete without an understanding of some business tools. Microsoft Excel and Access, Quickbooks, and the like are popular business tools that can play a valuable role in an information system, but an information system requires more than these applications. Necessary accounting architecture tools include servers, operating systems, databases, analysis and reporting software, etc. One purpose of the accounting architecture framework is to raise awareness of these less visible but vital information system technologies.

Control

The Control section also comprises four blocks:

  1. Security: protecting against unauthorized access.
  2. Confidentiality: safeguarding personal and corporate secrets.
  3. Availability: preventing data loss and system downtime.
  4. Processing Integrity: preventing data errors.

With the rise in cybersecurity threats, the need to safeguard data has become increasingly important, and a control framework teaches accountants how to safeguard data. These four blocks are the AICPA Trust Services Criteria (TSC). The TSC is not the only control framework, but it is an advantageous selection for the control section of the accounting architecture framework because it provides a concise focus on information system controls.

Not IT Specialists

The goal of this framework is not to encourage accountants to change their profession or to try to replace IT staff. Knowledge of accounting architecture creates accountants who can collaborate with IT staff to evaluate and use information systems that will provide the best possible information for decision makers. The goal is to “bridge the gap” between business specialists and IT specialists. Some have called this practice “business-led IT” because business specialists lead systems design. However, “accounting-led IT” may be more appropriate to emphasize the importance of accountants with their unique business knowledge collaborating with IT staff.

IT needs to find individuals who can work effectively with business teams to drive value to the enterprise. – Chris Bedi, CIO, ServiceNow

DevOps

Collaboration between IT and business specialists is vital because companies increasingly develop their information systems in house and need business expertise to know what data to collect and which information to visualize. This collaborative effort is often called “DevOps,” from the words development and operations. DevOps has some deeply technical implications for systems development, but on a high level, it serves as a reminder that cross-functional teams are the best solution for developing systems to meet stakeholders' informational needs. DevOps also highlights a number of important principles of effective engineers that also apply to effective accounting architects: Communication matters, Forget, “I don't know”, RTFM, and Search before asking.

Communication Matters
and Forget, “I Don't Know”

Collaboration is about communication. Many accountants may not use technical skills on a regular basis, but they need to communicate with those who do use technical skills. The ability to communicate and collaborate requires familiarity with IT tools, practices, and controls, but it also requires the ability to write and speak clearly and correctly.

“I don't know” is the verbal equivalent of admitting defeat. Every task as an opportunity (1) to seek out new knowledge, (2) to improve, and (3) to prove that we can enable the team to go further. Our thirst for knowledge should be unquenchable. We might not know it today, but we can know it tomorrow.

RTFM and Search Before Asking

The first, second, and fourth letters in RTFM stand for, “Read The Manual”. It is important not to ask how something works without reading its documentation first. Time is precious. Asking before reading wastes the time of those answering the question; it also wastes the time of the documentation writer who toiled to make the answer available in the first place.

He said, “Welcome to the freakin' Guardians of the Galaxy,” only he didn't use “freakin'.” – Rocket

The solutions to problems will not fall out of the sky. Solving new problems requires searching for new answers. We live in an amazing time; solutions to complex problems are at our fingertips. We just need to go and look for them.

Final Word

We're always looking for people who can have both a deep technology conversation, as well as a deep business conversation. You need the right blend of those people in your organization. But we also can't forget that technology is hard, and people who are very skilled at technology don't always have an interest in business processes. Having that right mix—and respecting them both appropriately—is a responsibility of the CIO and his team.
– Keith Collins, CIO, SAS

Understanding accounting architecture enables accountants to have deep business conversations and deep technology conversations, making accountants more valuable than ever before.
