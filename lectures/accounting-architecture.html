Introduction to Accounting Architecture

What Is an Accountant?

Accounting textbooks teach us that accounting is the measurement and communication of economic activities. This makes accountants the custodians of business information. Most accounting courses teach regulations to prepare students to become staff accountants or auditors or tax preparers. Although these are valuable skills, the role of information custodianship is much broader than tax returns, journal entries, and ledgers. Information custodianship involves understanding how to create information from the data that financial and non-financial business transactions generate, and accounting employers have begun to realize the need for a broader perspective of the definition of an accountant.

Demanded Competencies

What skills have accounting firms requested?

  • Data collection: the creation and acquisition of data.
  • Data analytics: the discovery of business information.
  • Data visualization: the reporting of business information to decision makers.

Information Is Money

What has precipitated the desire for these skills? Big Data. The world creates more than 2.5 quintillion bytes of data daily (1 quintillion bytes ≈ 1 exabyte ≈ 1 billion gigabytes) and the rate of data creation will continue to grow. More than 90% of all data is less than 5 years old. Understanding this data unlocks insights into customer behavior, economic trends, future strategies, etc.

He uses his power and wealth to gain information. The more he acquires, the greater his power and wealth.
– Sherlock Holmes on Charles Magnussen

The Information Era

The surge in technological innovation may lead some to believe erroneously that we live in the Technology Era, but the goal of technological innovation is not to serve technology. In other words, technology is not an end to itself. It is a means to an end, specifically a means to the generation of more information. Technology is a mere, albeit important, vessel for the generation of additional information. Make no mistake, we live in the Information Era.

Beyond Data Analytics

Data collection and analytics may be highly demanded skills, but information custodianship involves more than these. Custodianship begins with systems design, which involves the identification of the roles the information system should play, the data the system should collect, and the information or reports the system should generate, as well as the selection of technologies that build an information system to meet specifications.

All Types of Information?

Business information in an information system extends beyond traditional accounting content. Although the historical belief may have been that accountants should only care about accounting—to quote The Simpsons: “Hey, Surly only looks out for one guy... Surly!”—with the extensive business knowledge accountants have by virtue of their understanding of how to measure economic activities, they should expand their role to act as a custodian of all business information.

Old Meets New

Although the idea of systems design and general custodianship of business information is new to the accounting profession, the concept of supporting the information system is not. For years, one of the primary roles of accountants has been to audit IT systems to verify that existing systems meet control objectives to maintain data integrity and prevent unauthorized access. This has both positive and negative consequences. Because accountants have had a limited view of their role in the information system, they have been poorly equipped to serve as IT auditors. As accountants broaden their skills, not only will they be able to fill new and popular data analytics roles, rather they will also increase in their ability to perform the more traditional IT audit, and even financial audit, roles.

Accounting Architecture - Teaching the Skills

The purpose of this course is to train students to become accountants who can fill the spectrum of information custodian roles by explaining what an information system does, what it should be, and how it is controlled. The framework for this instruction is accounting architecture. The idea behind the name is that the framework explains the building blocks of an information system that supplies accounting information.

The Elephant in the Room

The title of this course in the catalog is “Accounting Systems”. In prior semesters this has caused confusion regarding the nature of this course. This course is more appropriately, and more traditionally, called “Accounting Information Systems” because the purpose is to explain the creation of accounting information. Perhaps, a better title would be “Accounting and Information Systems” to highlight that the course teaches the principles of an information system as they apply to accounting.

Not IT Specialists

The purpose of this course is not to encourage students to change their profession or to create accountants who can replace IT staff. Knowledge of accounting architecture creates accounting architects who can collaborate with IT staff to create and maintain information systems that will provide the best possible information for decision makers. The goal is to “bridge the gap” between business specialists and IT specialists. Some have called this practice “business-led IT” because business specialists lead systems design. I have coined the term “accounting-led IT” to emphasize the importance of accountants with their unique business knowledge collaborating with IT staff.

Accounting Architecture - The Sections

The arch of the accounting architecture has four sections: Foundation or Constraints, Information, Technology, and Control. The following slides introduce each of these sections, as well as the blocks that these sections comprise.

Constraints

Three forces determine the boundaries of a useful information system:

  • Compliance
  • Business Model
  • Environment

Think of these as the foundation of a house. The foundation is not sufficient to determine the best building schematics, but it can exclude many incompatible blueprints.

Compliance

The primary focus of the accounting curriculum is accounting rules and regulations. Financial accounting, audit, and tax are rules-based, and any useful information system must generate information necessary to comply with these rules (e.g., financial statements, tax forms, etc.).

Compliance also addresses contractual relationships with lenders, suppliers, and customers. Although these are not regulatory bodies, they may demand access to certain information or a certain level of security.

Business Model

Entrepreneurs and operations specialists generate business ideas and the model to execute those ideas. Accountants and information systems specialists report on the chosen model. As the information system collects data and reports on the existing business model, accountants can provide insights into potential modifications of the current model, as well as entirely new strategies. (Big Data analytics may be particularly useful for divining new strategies.)

Environment

The external and internal environment set the boundaries for what an information system and an organization, in general, can and should do. The external environment comprises economic and market forces, as well as external technological constraints, and the internal environment comprises the core competencies, management ability, corporate culture, financial resources, and internal technological constraints of the firm. External technological constraints are the boundaries of IT capabilities. Internal technological constraints are the abilities within an organization to develop unique information system components (e.g., write software) that will adapt the information system to the organization's unique business model and information needs.

Feedback

The external and internal environment and the other two foundational blocks, compliance and business model, are interrelated. The external environment can affect the need for and level of required compliance. Both the external and internal environments stipulate the set of available business models (i.e., business strategies), as well as the optimal strategy. Furthermore, a well-defined information system continuously receives data regarding the current state of the external and internal environments. This data feeds into strategic decision making that further modifies the chosen business model.

Information

The Information section of the arch comprises three blocks:

  1. Creation: data collection or acquisition and entry into the system.
  2. Use: data analysis and reporting.
  3. Maintenance: data storage and preservation.

Each of these three blocks contains multiple steps. Together they form the information life cycle (i.e., what the information systems should do). Current employers have focused their attention on data analysis, but although Use is the culminating block of the Information section and the architecture model as a whole, knowledge of data analysis tools is incomplete without an understanding of the data and the process by which it becomes information.

Technology

The Technology section comprises four blocks:

  1. Hardware: the physical components of the information system.
  2. Software: operating systems and server- and client-side applications.
  3. Storage: physical and logical data storage.
  4. Services: networking and cloud computing.

Whereas the Information section discusses the principles of information, the Technology blocks represent the physical manifestation of the information system (i.e., what the information system should be).

What About “Legacy Technologies”?

PwC lists Microsoft Excel and Access as “legacy technologies,” and they continue to be valuable, especially Excel. However, these are “business tools” and not information technologies. As a result, they are not the best tools for data storage or analysis, although they are frequently used for these purposes. In this course, we will focus on enterprise-grade information systems, and we will not discuss these legacy business tools.

What About Quickbooks, etc?

Small and large businesses have different informational needs, different technological needs, and different abilities to hire IT staff to manage information systems. As a result, small firms may rely on small-business accounting applications, such as Quickbooks. Expertise with these tools may be useful in working as a bookkeeper in a small business, but the focus of this course is not on bookkeeping tools.

Controls

The Controls section also comprises four blocks:

  1. Security: protecting against unauthorized access.
  2. Availability: preventing data loss and system downtime.
  3. Processing Integrity: preventing data errors.
  4. Confidentiality: safeguarding personal and corporate secrets.

These four blocks are the AICPA Trust Services Framework. Although many other control frameworks exist, I have selected this because it focuses on data security (i.e., how the system is controlled). With the rise in cybersecurity threats, information security has become increasingly important, and accountants are particularly interested in compliance with good control practices.

Accounting Tie-Ins

Some students feel that these IT-related topics are not relevant to them and that they would prefer to focus on accounting regulations. I accept that it is my responsibility to prove otherwise. As a result, in each lecture I will try to include “Accounting Tie-in” slides in an attempt to emphasize how accounting architecture “places the components of the information systems in context with accounting regulations,” or in other words, how an accounting architecture principle matters to accountants.

Communication Matters

Many accountants, even accounting architects, will not use technical skills on a regular basis. The role of accountants is to collaborate with IT staff on the design, maintenance, and audit of information systems using words. The ability to communicate and collaborate requires familiarity with IT tools, practices, and controls, but it also requires the ability to write and speak clearly with proper spelling and grammar.

The “Big” Tie-in

I developed accounting architecture because employers want accountants with the ability to understand data and audit information systems.

  • Two Big 4 accounting firms have proposed additions to the current curriculum covering information system technologies.
  • Partners from KPMG and PwC have expressed interest in hiring more accountants with IT knowledge.

Effective Fall 2015, Fogelman offers an additional MS in Accounting track for data analytics, as well as a certificate in Business Intelligence and Analysis. Even if only some students pursue this career path, this modification to the curriculum will improve these individuals' preparation and future opportunities.
