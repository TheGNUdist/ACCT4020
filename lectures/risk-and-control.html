Risk & Control

Accounting Architecture

Information is the keystone of any information system, and Technology is the set of tools necessary to implement that system. Control, however, is a choice. A system can function without proper controls, but a system without proper controls exposes internal and external stakeholders to risk. Entities cannot choose the risks, but they can choose how to manage them.

Risky Business

Risks can take on many forms. Some risks target the business indirectly, such as economic risk and financial risk. Examples of economic risk are:

  • Industry competition
  • Technological innovation
  • Changes in tax, legal, or regulatory standards
  • Economic trends
  • Interest rates and availability of financing
  • Energy and fuel costs
  • Customer preferences
  • Demand cyclicality

Adapt or Perish

Several companies have failed because of an inability to thrive amid economic risk. One prominent example is the long list of construction companies that failed during the Great Recession of 2008 when consumers could no longer receive mortgage loans and home prices plummeted. Other examples include:

  • Blockbuster: Failed to transition to streaming
  • Kodak: Failed to understand importance of digital photography and photo sharing
  • Nokia & Motorola: Failed to apply popularity of feature phones to smartphone arena
  • HP & Palm: Failed to compete in mobile computing market
  • Borders: Failed to recognize the rise of ebooks

Better Knowledge

In several cases, businesses failed under the pressure of economic risk because of an unwillingness or inability to change their business model following technological innovation. The accounting profession finds itself in a similar predicament. One solution to this problem is a willingness to accept change and not dig in when innovation occurs. The second solution is better information. Well-designed information systems can help companies manage economic risks by improving forecasts, maximizing business process efficiency, and supporting adaptation of strategic goals. Therefore, accountants have a choice: be a part of the problem by ignoring the risk or be a part of the solution be and provide others with the information necessary to manage the risk.

Coming For You

Other risks target the business entity directly. Some of these are external threats, and some are internal threats. Today, the most prominent external threats are cyber incidents, frequently referred to as cyber attacks or hacking. These are considered external risks because they are generally initiated by outsiders. Internal threats, perpetrated by current or former employees, can be either intentional (i.e., fraud) or unintentional (i.e., error). Regardless of the source, these risks can result in data loss, corruption, or leakage and asset misappropriation. Additionally, certain internal risks can result in material misstatement of accounting numbers.

Black Hat Tools

Hackers have a plethora of tools in their arsenal to engage in cyber attacks. Some tools rely on malware, which stands for malicious software, while others Some of the more common examples are:

  • Virus
  • Worm
  • Trojan horse
  • Ransomware: Malware that encrypts the user's computer forcing the user to pay for a decryption key

Sony

Sony is no stranger to cyber incidents. In April 2011, the PlayStation Network experienced a month-long outage following an attack that leaked 77 million accounts and over 12,000 credit card numbers. At the time it was viewed as the largest breach in history, and Sony estimated outage costs at $171 million. This number excludes the cost of litigation, fines, and consumer credit protection that Sony provided to all account holders.

Then, in November 2014, a hacker group leaked data from Sony Pictures including employee information, private emails, and corporate documents and unreleased films. Sony has paid millions in connection with this attack.

Yahoo!

Yahoo is the grand champion of cyber incidents. In 2013 and 2014 combined, two data breaches affected over 1.5 billion accounts. Each breach by itself is larger than any breach experience by any other company in the history of the Internet. In both cases, hackers leaked account information and security data, including security questions and answers. Yahoo announced the breaches in 2016 after Verizon had made an offer to acquire Yahoo for $4.8 billion. Verizon subsequently reduced the price by $350 million. Additionally, Yahoo CEO Marissa Mayer forfeited bonuses for 2016 and 2017. Additional litigation is currently pending.

Ashley Madison

In August 2015, a group of hackers called “The Impact Team” leaked data from Avid Life Media Inc.'s website Ashley Madison that it had stolen one month prior in an attempt to blackmail the company into shutting down its service. The leaked data included corporate emails and the personal information of current and former users. Some of the former users had already paid to have their accounts deleted, but ALM failed to delete their data, even though the company claimed to have done so. As a result of this leak, ALM face a $568 million class-action lawsuit.

Credit Card Hack

Several retailers have experienced breaches of credit card data in recent years. Two of the most prominent members of this list are Target with 70 million customers affected and Home Depot with 56 million customers affected. In both cases, hackers remotely accessed and infected point-of-sale (POS) systems by using stolen login credentials from vendors. POS systems are a popular target for credit card theft, and several other retailers have also been affected, such as Wendy's, Staples, UPS, P.F. Chang's, Sally Beauty. However, in the case of the largest credit card leak, 94 million T. J. Maxx customers, hackers stole the credit card numbers from the corporate system simply by hacking the wireless network at a single store.

WannaCry

Cyber incidents can result in asset misappropriation without actually stealing anything. Thanks to an error in the Server Message Protocol used by Microsoft Windows to share files, in May 2017, attackers were able to infect over 200,000 computers worldwide with WannaCry ransomware in a single day. The infected computers included as many as 70,000 National Health Service hospital devices in Great Britain, and as a result, emergency rooms were forced to turn patients away. Part of the problem was that many computers ran Windows XP and Windows Server 2003, which no longer receive security updates. However, the majority of affected devices ran current versions of Windows, and Microsoft had simply not chosen to fix this error despite being aware of it.

Dyn

Dyn provides Domain Name System (DNS) services from the northeastern United States. On October 21, 2016, unprecedented number of IoT devices infected by Mirai rendered Dyn unavailable for several hours. As a result, several websites were inaccessible by their URLs, including Twitter, Netflix, and Amazon. This outage cost these companies tens of thousands of dollars every minute, and immediately following the outage, Dyn lost 8% of its customer base as companies switched their traffic to other DNS providers. Although Dyn was the victim of this attack, the blame more appropriately lies with the manufacturers and owners of the infected IoT devices. However the loss from cyber attacks is indiscriminate. Even those not at fault lose.

Fraud Triangle

The fraud triangle highlights the three forces that lead to fraudulent behavior, including cyber attacks. Both outsiders and insiders are subject to pressure and rationalization, but although cyber incidents by outsiders are the most common cause of data breaches, firm insiders are also able to cause significant damage through fraud because they can have greater opportunity to leak internal data.

Snowden & Manning

Because of their ability to access data through legitimate channels, malicious insiders can pose a considerable risk. With respect to US government and military documents, ex-CIA employee Edward Snowden and US Army Specialist Bradley Manning both released classified data to the public. In 2010, Manning assisted Julian Assange, founder of WikiLeaks, in publishing documents and videos on the Iraq and Afghanistan wars, as well as Guantanamo Bay, and in 2013, Snowden leaked thousands of documents on NSA surveillance programs to reporters from various news outlets.

Facebook

Erroneous data leaks by insiders can be just as bad as intentional ones. In 2016, Facebook discovered that an error in their popular social media software revealed the personal profiles of content moderators to Facebook groups whose administrators the moderators had banned. This error exposed the personal profiles of more than 1,000 Facebook employees. Approximately 40 of these employees worked for Facebook's counter-terrorism unit in Facebook's European headquarters, and their profiles were leaked to the very terrorist groups they worked to ban. At least one of these employees went into hiding when he discovered that members of a terrorist group that he had worked against had viewed his profile.

What's Best for Business

Data breaches are bad business. According to one report, in 2015 over 700 million records were breached in nearly 1,700 cyber incidents. That equates to 22 records leaked every second. 62% of cyber incidents were initiated by outsiders, 14% were initiated by malicious insiders, and 24% were accidental. Experian predicts that insider fraud and error will continue to be a significant contributor to data leaks. In 2014, US companies reported $40 billion in losses stemming from breaches caused by employees. Currently, companies lose billions of dollars more because of externally initiated cyber incidents, and by 2010, data breach costs may reach as high as $2 trillion worldwide.

Internal Controls

In the same sense that better information can help companies manage market risks, internal controls can help companies manage fraud and error risks. Internal controls are activities or tools designed to protect data, assets, business processes, and compliance objectives (e.g., financial reporting) from error and fraud. In the context of the fraud triangle, internal controls primarily focus on reducing the opportunity to commit fraud (as well as cause unintentional errors), but certain control objectives also address perceived pressure and rationalization.

COSO

The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission (COSO) has created a framework for designing and implementing internal controls that “achieve the entity's objectives and adapt to changes in the business and operating environments.” COSO identifies three control objectives, which map directly into the Environment section of accounting architecture. Operations falls under Business Model, and Reporting and Compliance both fall under Compliance.

Control Weakness

Unfortunately, internal controls suffer from one significant weakness: inefficiency.

Cost-Benefit Analysis

The benefit of a control implementation is the reduction in either the likelihood of or exposure to problems. Likelihood is the probability of a problem, and exposure is the dollar loss from litigation, fines, lost business, cost of restoring a system and its data, etc., should a problem occur. Preventive controls can lower the likelihood of a problem, and detective and corrective controls can reduce the exposure (i.e., make a problem less bad or fix a problem more quickly). Any implementation of controls is costly, and although 100% prevention would be the best, the cost is prohibitive. As a result, the only option is a combination of all three types: preventing what can be prevented and detecting and correcting what cannot be prevented.

Benefit to Internal Stakeholders

Decision makers within the firm are the primary beneficiary of internal controls. Controls reduce the likelihood of misinformation, which would lead to incorrect strategic decisions, and they protect the confidentiality of trade secrets. Because internal stakeholders are the biggest “customer” of the information system, protecting them from risks becomes a primary goal of information system controls. Additionally, because firms can face serious consequences from external stakeholders in the event of a breach or outage, internal stakeholders also benefit from controls that protect the firm from litigation.

Benefit to External Stakeholders

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX) requires firms to implement internal controls over financial reporting to prevent material misstatements. Congress instituted this statute in response to the egregious accounting frauds in the lates 90s and early 2000s at Enron, Worldcom, and others. SOX sections 302 and 404 require management to maintain and assess internal controls over financial reporting. SOX complements GAAP and GAAS in protecting external stakeholders from financial misinformation.

Other regulations and private contracts also internal controls to prevent breaches of privacy, data loss, and system unavailability. As a result, Compliance often requires the use of internal controls, regardless of the intrinsic benefit to internal stakeholders.

Process Controls

Segregation of Duties

Segregation of duties discourages the assignment of multiple incompatible duties to the same individual. General guidelines for identifying incompatible duties include:

  1. Authorization for a transaction should be separate from the processing of a transaction. For example, a sales person should not be able to authorize sale of inventory on credit and process the sales transaction. This prevents that creation of fraudulent transactions.
  2. Custody of assets should be separate from record-keeping. For example, the raw materials store should not be responsible for maintaining records of the levels of raw materials. This prevents asset theft.

Supervision

Segregation of duties is an excellent tool for discouraging fraud by requiring individuals to collude in order to succeed in performing and concealing the act. However, sometimes this tool is not available either because the number of available employees is too small and employees need to assume incompatible duties or because a single duty is risky (e.g., unloading inventory from a truck before it is counted or opening envelopes of cash in the mail room). In these situations, supervision is a second-best alternative.

Trust Services

Trust Services has a foundation and four pillars:

  • Security protects against unauthorized access.
  • Availability prevents data loss and system downtime.
  • Processing integrity preserves data integrity.
  • Confidentiality and privacy maintain secrets. Because of their similarity, these two occupy the same block in the Architecture.
