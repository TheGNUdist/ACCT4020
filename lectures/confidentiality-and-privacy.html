Accounting Architecture

Confidentiality and privacy combine to form the second Control block in the accounting architecture framework. Unlike the other blocks in the Control section, this block comprises two related Trust Services Criteria with a single overarching goal: to prevent unauthorized access to secret information. Although unauthorized access is also the purview of security, confidentiality and privacy address risks and control activities that extend beyond what security addresses.

Trust Services Criteria

Information designated as confidential is protected as committed or agreed. Personal information is collected, used, retained, disclosed, and disposed to meet the entity's objectives.

Protection means to keep secret data from being read, modified, or used by unauthorized individuals. Three tools serve to preserve data privacy and confidentiality:

  • Rights management avoids unlicensed use of intellectual property.
  • Intermediation anonymizes communication.
  • Encryption prevents eavesdropping and tampering and enables authentication.

Confidentiality and Privacy

The two Trust Services Criteria that the Confidentiality block comprises are Confidentiality and Privacy. The AICPA explains the difference between these two terms. “Confidentiality is distinguished from privacy in that privacy deals with personal information, whereas confidentiality refers to a broader range of information that is not restricted to personal information.” From this explanation it is clear that the same tools protect privacy and confidentiality.

Confidentiality/Privacy and Security

As the foundation of the Trust Services framework, Security plays a role in promoting all other criteria, especially confidentiality. In fact, a primary focus of Security (i.e., access controls) is to keep data secret. Because of the similarity between the concepts of Security and Confidentiality, many tools designed to preserve confidentiality are commonly referred to as security tools. The difference between these criteria is the precise methods used to prevent unauthorized access.

Confidentiality/Privacy and Compliance

Internal controls protect digital resources in the information system owned by either the firm itself or third parties. As a result, Compliance is particularly applicable to the Confidentiality & Privacy block of the architecture. From the perspective of accountants, much of Compliance revolves around financial accounting, auditing, and tax regulations. However, Compliance also includes the laws and private contracts that dictate the protection of trade secrets, customer data, intellectual property, etc.

Rights Management

Intellectual property licenses dictate approved use of licensed content. Maintenance of rights metadata prevents unauthorized access and ensures prompt deletion of content with expired licenses. As a result, rights management is a required compliance activity when using licensed content. This practice applies not only to proprietary content, but also to open source. Because open source licenses are licenses, rights management is necessary to comply with the license requirements. This frequently involves correct attribution (i.e., including the name of the original creator with the product) and reciprocation (i.e., using a license for internal modifications that is compatible with the original license).

Intermediation

Intermediation involves one party interacting with another via a middleman. The decision to rely on intermediation to communicate or transact business can have multiple motivations. One motivation is convenience. For example, information intermediaries, such as debt and equity analysts, communicate information regarding firm performance to investors so that these investors will not need to engage in their own financial statement analysis. Another motivation is reliability. Financial intermediaries funnel funds from one group to another when the groups cannot trust each other. One frequent motivation is anonymity. A human intermediary can hide the identity of the party requesting access to resources. Similarly, a digital intermediary can hide the identity of a user requesting access to Internet resources.

Review of the WAN

In order to access Internet (i.e., WAN) resources, a device must have a public IP address. Because of network address translation (NAT), this IP address can differ from a device's LAN address, but the public IP address is the address that the device uses to access the Internet. Similar to caller ID, when a device accesses an Internet resource (e.g., visiting a Web site), the server that hosts that resource records the public IP address of the client device in a log file. This record exposes some aspects of the identity of the user. Because the public IP can be traced to the geographic location of the client, logs can identify where the client device is, which ISP the client used to connect to the Internet, and, if applicable, on which company's LAN the device resides.

Proxy Servers

Proxy servers serve as intermediaries between a user and a Web server in order to hide details about the client. The client selects and connects to a proxy server, and while connected, the proxy server forwards all Internet requests from the client to the Web server and content from the Web server to the client. The Web server records the IP address of the proxy server, instead of the public IP address of the client. Attempts to identify the location or ISP of the client would prove fruitless, as the proxy server may be in a different country as the actual client. Perpetrators of illicit activities (e.g., hacking or the sale or purchase of contraband) use this feature to evade discovery. Others use remote proxy servers in order to anonymize country of origin and access restricted digital content available only to the residents of another country.

Proxy Servers and Firewalls

Firewalls keep unauthorized WAN users from accessing LAN servers, but they can also keep LAN users from accessing certain WAN resources. For example, if a university wanted to prevent its employees from accessing social media sites, a firewall could block access to these sites from university computers. Proxy servers can circumvent these restrictions because to the university's firewall the client only accessed a proxy server, not a social media site.

Put to a Good Use

Corporations also use proxy servers in other ways to promote confidentiality and convenience. First, forcing employees to access the Internet through a proxy server effectively anonymizes their Internet activity by masking the IP address of origin. Second, if users inside a corporate LAN need to access the same WAN resources (e.g., a Web site) frequently, the proxy server can create a cache (i.e., a temporary copy) of that resource so that the clients can access it more quickly. Third, although a proxy server can hide the IP address of the client from the Internet server, the proxy server itself records the IP address of the client, as well as the traffic to and from the client. This provides the corporation an easy method to track the Internet resources their employees have accessed.

VPN

Virtual private network (VPN) servers are similar to proxy servers in that they serve as an intermediary between a client and the Internet. However, VPN servers have a particular advantage over proxy servers, and that is that VPN servers use encryption to protect the communication between VPN server and client. As a result, VPN is a popular method for giving teleworkers secure access to corporate networks in order to access file shares, databases, or other servers.

Eavesdropping

Because the standard for network communication is plain text, anyone who can intercept a message (e.g., requesting a Web page, entering data into username and password or form fields, etc.) can read it. Strong RADIUS authentication protocols restrict access to a LAN in order to prevent network sniffing on the LAN, but anyone can connect to the Internet and sniff (i.e., eavesdrop) on communication between clients and Internet servers. Access controls cannot prevent unauthorized access to communication over the Internet. By encrypting communication to and from a client, VPN servers ensure that anyone who eavesdrops will not be able to interpret the communication. Consequently, VPNs are also a popular choice for preserving confidentiality when using public Wi-Fi networks (e.g., coffee shops).

Wi-Fi Protected Access

Wired Equivalent Privacy (WEP) and Wi-Fi Protected Access (WPA) also use encryption to limit eavesdropping on wireless networks. These methods require a user to use a key or a password to connect to a wireless network, and that key or password then encrypts any network traffic between the user's device and router. This is one reason why secure wireless networks are so much safer than open wireless networks. WEP proved insecure because it was easy for an eavesdropper to calculate the encrpytion key. WPA, currently WPA2, although WPA3 is being implemented, replaced WEP as a more secure alternative. However, users with devices on the same WPA-secured network all have the password and can use it to eavesdrop on each other. Connecting to a VPN server can protect devices on a shared wireless network, even if the network is protected with WPA.

Encryption and Decryption

Encryption uses a key to convert plain text into cipher text, which is unreadable by humans and computers. The key, sometimes referred to as a cipher, is a string of characters and an algorithm that determines a transformation of plain text to obtain cipher text. Enterprise-grade data encryption keys rely on complicated mathematics but the principles are no different from creating cipher text by shifting the letters in a message by one (i.e., a becomes b, b becomes c, etc.). In order to read an encrypted message, the recipient must have the decryption key, a string of characters and an algorithm to convert cipher text back into plain text. As long as the decryption key is protected from unauthorized recipients, communication is secure.

Two Forms of Encryption

Encryption keys can either be symmetric or asymmetric. In symmetric, or private key, encryption, the encryption and decryption keys are the same, and both the sender and the recipient must have the same key. The difficulty with this form of encryption is that the sender must find a way to share the decryption key with the recipient without it falling into unauthorized hands. In asymmetric, or public key, encryption, the key to encrypt the data is publicly distributed and a paired private key is used for decryption. The sender encrypts the message with the recipient's public key, and the recipient decrypts the message with the recipient's own private key. Public key encryption is more secure because users never share private keys, but this method of encryption takes longer and both encryption and decryption require more processing power.

Public Key Cryptography

Imagine Alice wants to tell Bob a secret without letting Eve know the secret. Alice encrypts the secret message with Bob's public key and sends the cypher text to Bob. Bob uses his private key to decrypt Alice's message. Anyone with Bob's public key can encrypt a message, but only the holder of Bob's private key can decrypt the message. This is how cryptography promotes confidentiality.

Oversharing

Sharing public keys in public key cryptography is safe for two reasons. Using the analogy of a safe, the public key places a lock on the safe to seal it shut. Only one key fits the lock and that is the private key. An unauthorized user with another lock (i.e., another copy of the public key) cannot tamper with or read the message, nor can that user guess the decryption key from studying the lock. An email account provides another analogy to illustrate this point. A public key is like an email address. In order to receive email, the recipient must distribute the address. This is a secure behavior because the email address is not sufficient to read received emails. Viewing emails requires an additional input: a password which the owner of the account keeps secret. Knowing an email address is not enough to guess the password.

Cryptographic Hash

Even though cipher text is not human- or computer-readable, it can still provide useful information even when decryption is not possible. In fact, some encryption protocols are designed to convert data into a short cipher text that cannot be decrypted. This is called one-way encryption and the cipher text is called a cryptographic hash or a checksum. The purpose of a hash is not to protect a message in transit but rather to verify the integrity of the data. Because hash encryption protocols use a single key that is common to all users of the protocol, any attempt to create a hash of the same content will result in the same hash. However, even a slight modification to the data will result in a different hash.

Tamper-proof

Calculating a hash after receiving a file and comparing it to a hash that the sender separately provides will reveal whether the file has been modified. For example, if a hacker gains access to and adds a virus to an online file, then users who download and launch the file can become infected. If the creator of the file provides a hash of the original file and users calculate a hash of the downloaded file, they would learn that the file had become corrupted and delete it.

Hashing

Imagine Bob wants to send Alice a message, but Eve might change the message. Bob can send the message and a hash of the message. When Alice receives the message, she can hash the same message to see whether she obtains the same hash. If the hash is different, the message has been changed. This is how cryptography promotes integrity.

When Worlds Collide

Hashing has a flaw. The total number of potential hashes is much smaller than the total number of different files imaginable. As a result, multiple files must have the same hash at some point. A collision is when two different files generate the same hash. Once a hashing algorithm causes a collision, it should no longer be used.

Digital Signature

Another benefit of encrypted cipher text is its ability to identify the sender. Because a public-private key pair is unique to a single user, they can confirm the sender's identity by allowing the sender to attach a digital signature to the message. (This is different from the “digital signature” that cardholders draw on a screen after swiping a credit card.) A sender creates a cryptographic digital signature by (1) creating a hash of the message and (2) encrypting the hash with the sender's own private key.

Verify the Signature

The sender then transmits the message and the signature to the recipient. The recipient (1) creates a hash of the message, (2) decrypts the signature using the sender's public key and (3) compares the hash with the decrypted signature. If they are equal, then the sender's identity is confirmed and the message has not been modified in transit. If they are different, then either the message has been modified or the sender is not who the sender claims to be.

Who Are You?

Unfortunately, the recipient of a message cannot know that the owner of the private key is who the owner claims to be in real life. What's to stop Eve from creating a public-private key pair and sign a message claiming to be Bob? The public key that Eve shares claiming to be Bob will verify the digital signature, and Alice will think that she is communicating with Bob. Someone else needs to verify that the public key that Bob shares is really Bob and the public key that Eve shares is not Bob. The solution is for a trusted agent to verify Bob's identity. The trusted agent, called a certificate authority (CA) issues a certificate that identifies the public key that was created by the real Bob. Alice can know which public key is Bob's by reading the certificate associated with any public key claiming to be from Bob. This is how cryptography promotes authentication.

HTTPS and SFTP

Although it is possible to use a computer to create keys, hashes, cipher text, and signatures manually, these processes frequently occur transparently for the end user. For example, when a Web site relies on Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) or Transport Layer Security (TLS) to establish encrypted communication with a client-side browser, the end user is completely unaware of the transmission of keys. Sometimes, the only salient evidence of the encryption is the “HTTPS” schema with the URL, which is the encrypted alternative to the HTTP protocol. The FTP schema also has a secure alternative (SFTP). SFTP uses similar encryption methods for communication with a file server as HTTPS uses for communication with an HTTP server (i.e., a server that hosts a Web page).

Secure Shell

VPN provides encrypted access to any number of WAN and LAN resources and is frequently used to provide teleworkers access to private corporate networks and clouds. Another popular tool for encrypted communication with remote computers is Secure Shell (SSH). As the name implies, SSH provides access to a text-based shell on a remote computer. This is comparable to using the terminal on a UNIX-like operating system, except that the commands are run against a remote computer, instead of the local machine. This is the current standard for accessing the command line of another computer. SSH is commonly considered the secure alternative to telnet, which historically was the standard for communication between two computers.

Man-In-The-Middle

The combination of keys and certificates protects Internet users from man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks, in which a malicious server fraudulently poses as a trusted server (e.g., a fake version of Amazon.com) in order to intercept data meant for the trusted server and obtain confidential information. Encryption keys do not protect data from these attacks because the user communicates with the fraudulent server using the fraudulent server's keys. Because a certificate authority would not be fooled by a fraudulent server, only the trusted server will have the certificate associated with the trusted server's address. Web browsers automatically check the certificate of an HTTPS site to confirm that it matches the URL and that it was issued by a trusted certificate authority.

Stationary

Many, and perhaps most, examples of digital encryption address network communication, but this is not the only valuable use. Encryption can also serve to secure stored data. One of the reasons why unauthorized physical access is dangerous is that although an unauthorized user cannot log in to a workstation, that same user can steal the hard drive and connect it to another device in order to read confidential data. Storage volume encryption can alleviate this concern by encrypting all content on a drive. An authorized user can enter a password while booting the computer in order to decrypt the volume. Once the volume is decrypted, it functions as an ordinary storage volume until the user shuts down the computer.

Cryptocurrency

One interesting use of public key cryptography is in cryptocurrency, which is a form of digital currency that relies on multiple forms of cryptography to create a decentralized banking system that is independent of any central authority. Bitcoin is the most well-known variant, but many others exist. In cryptocurrency, each transaction (i.e., payment) is digitally signed by the private key of the payer to prove ownership of the coins, and each payment identifies its payee by the payee's public key. Cryptography serves multiple purposes. First, private keys demonstrate authenticity and authorization to spend coins, and second, public keys establish anonymity because no method currently exists to identify the real-world identity of a payee by public key.

Blockchain

Cryptography also serves a third purpose in cryptocurrency: maintaining the integrity of the transaction ledger. The blockchain is a database that records all cryptocurrency transactions. Each block in the chain is a collection of transactions, and the entire chain allows users to track account balances by netting all receipts and payments. No other record of account balances exists. In order to maintain the veracity of the ledger and prevent users from retroactively modifying prior transactions, each block is connected to the previous block in the chain by cryptographic hash. Each block has a hash, and that hash includes the transactions in the block, as well as the hash of the previous block. Any attempt to modify a transaction would change the hash of a block, as well as the hash of all subsequent blocks making the blockchain almost immutable.

Ransomware

Just as proxy servers can serve to hide the identities of hackers or traffickers in contraband, cryptography can also be put to nefarious purposes. On the one hand, cryptography can hide the identities of black market participants in a cryptocurrency transaction, and it can also encrypt messages that contain harmful information. On the other hand, hackers can also use cryptography in cyber attacks. Public key cryptography enables ransomware to lock users out of their data. Attackers then demand a ransom, frequently in cryptocurrency because it preserves their identity, in order to turn over the private key that decrypts the data. Each day the ransom increases, and if users fail to pay, the ransomware begins to delete their data.

Is Cryptography the Future?

Businesses care about keeping data confidential, and regulations require that employee, patient, and customer data remain private. Even in the absence of these regulations, media coverage of past data breaches demonstrates the harrowing effects of the leakage of confidential data. Proper tools and controls enable systems to satisfy regulatory compliance, client expectations, and business needs. Cryptography is a powerful tool for protecting confidentiality and privacy, and users have now begun to investigate whether it can do more to promote data integrity. The blockchain has successfully served as a secure database of cryptocurrency transactions, and the financial, insurance, and accounting professions have investigated whether this ledger technology could replace traditional ledgers in other settings, as well.
